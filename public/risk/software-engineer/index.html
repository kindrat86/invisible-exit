Side Business for Software Engineers — Risk & Stealth Guide

Can Software Engineers build a side business while employed? Complete non-compete analysis, salary replacement targets, and stealth strategies for 2026.

Risk Level: Low in ban states, medium elsewhere. Most software engineers can build micro-SaaS without triggering non-compete enforcement as long as the product is in a different vertical from their employer.

Salary & Replacement Target

Median Software Engineer Salary: $145,000 (median, BLS Q1 2026)
Monthly Replacement Target: $12,083/mo

Your side business needs to replace this amount to achieve financial independence from your employer. Use the freedom number calculator to model your exit timeline.

Non-Compete Enforceability

California (unenforceable for employees), Colorado (requires notice + compensation), Washington (restricted to high-earners >$120K), Illinois (banned for low-wage). Texas and New York enforce narrowly.

Safest States: California, Colorado, Oklahoma, North Dakota, Minnesota, and D.C. — these states either ban or severely restrict non-competes for employees. If you live in one of these states, your employer non-compete is likely unenforceable for a side business.

How to Stay Invisible

Use a Delaware LLC with a registered agent (don't register in your home state if enforcing). Keep your GitHub contributions private. Use a separate email, Stripe account, and domain registration with WHOIS privacy. Never use employer equipment or work hours.

Best Micro-SaaS Ideas for Software Engineers

AI wrapper tools ($5K-15K MRR potential), developer tools, API-as-a-service, Chrome extensions, SaaS boilerplates. Software engineers have the highest success rate in micro-SaaS because they build without paying developer costs.

Tax Entity Recommendation

LLC (default) or S-Corp election at >$60K profit. Delaware LLC most common for stealth purposes — no public member disclosure.

Disclaimer: This is educational content, not legal advice. Non-compete laws vary by state and individual employment contract. Consult an employment attorney in your state before starting a side business.
